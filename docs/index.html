Abstract

Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) are overparameterized by design — a feature, not a bug. But can we surgically remove most of their parameters and still preserve the signal they've memorized? This work applies the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis to SIREN networks fitted to a single image, contrasting three pruning strategies across 18 iterative rounds down to 1.8% of original network weight. We find that SIREN contains a remarkable winning ticket — a 2.3% sub-network maintaining 32.7 dB PSNR — while the losing ticket collapses catastrophically to noise in just two pruning rounds. The mechanism is not bandwidth limitation but frequency carrier destruction: SIREN's high-magnitude weights act as the primary oscillators of its Fourier decomposition, and removing them is equivalent to silencing all radio stations simultaneously.

01 — Introduction

The Overparameterization Paradox

There is a strange tension at the heart of modern deep learning. We build networks far larger than they need to be — and it helps. Overparameterization aids optimization, improves generalization, and opens basins of attraction that underfitted architectures could never reach. Yet the moment training ends, we are left with a bloated artifact that spends most of its compute multiplying numbers by near-zero weights. This is the overparameterization paradox: the redundancy that makes learning possible makes deployment expensive.

For most computer vision tasks, this is merely an engineering inconvenience. But for Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) — networks that replace discrete data arrays with continuous learned functions — overparameterization is existential. An INR representing a 256×256 image stores roughly 262,000 floats. The uncompressed image itself is 196,608 floats. The network is already larger than what it models. If we cannot compress INRs, their promise of "infinite-resolution, differentiable storage" rings hollow in any real deployment.

The question I wanted to answer: does the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (LTH) extend to SIREN? And if it does, what makes a neuron "load-bearing" in a network whose architecture is built around periodic functions rather than rectified linear units?

The answer turned out to be simultaneously reassuring and disturbing — reassuring because yes, winning tickets exist and they're remarkably small; disturbing because the losing tickets don't just degrade — they undergo instantaneous frequency collapse, a failure mode so distinctive it tells us something fundamental about how SIRENs encode information.

Personal Note

I started this project convinced the SIREN/LTH intersection would behave like any other MLP experiment — gradual degradation across the board. The speed of the loser's collapse genuinely surprised me. It's one of those results where you check for bugs twice before accepting it as real.

02 — Background

Two Ideas That Collide

The Spectral Bias Problem and SIREN's Solution

The core problem with using standard ReLU networks as implicit representations is well-documented: neural networks exhibit spectral bias (Rahaman et al., 2019), learning low-frequency components of a target function first, often never fully capturing high-frequency detail regardless of training duration. For natural images — which derive most of their perceptual richness from edges, textures, and high-frequency patterns — this is fatal.

Sitzmann et al. (2020) proposed an elegant solution: replace ReLU with $\sin(\omega_0 \cdot)$. The resulting Sinusoidal Representation Network (SIREN) has no spectral bias because every layer is already a Fourier basis function. Through composition of sine functions, the network can represent arbitrarily complex spectra. The key insight — stated in the paper but often underappreciated — is that any derivative of a SIREN is itself a SIREN, since $\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x) = \cos(x)$ is a phase-shifted sine. This makes SIRENs uniquely suited for physics-based applications where the gradient field is as important as the function itself.

$$\Phi(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{W}_n \left( \phi_{n-1} \circ \phi_{n-2} \circ \cdots \circ \phi_0 \right)(\mathbf{x}) + \mathbf{b}_n$$
$$\phi_i(\mathbf{x}_i) = \sin\!\left(\omega_0 \cdot \mathbf{W}_i \mathbf{x}_i + \mathbf{b}_i\right)$$
SIREN forward pass: each layer applies an affine transform scaled by $\omega_0$ and wrapped in sine. The first layer uses $\omega_0 = 30$ to set the initial frequency bandwidth; subsequent layers inherit it.

The initialization scheme is equally critical. Preserving activation statistics across layers requires sampling weights from $\mathcal{U}\!\left(-\sqrt{6/n}, \sqrt{6/n}\right) / \omega_0$ for hidden layers, derived by ensuring each layer's output distribution remains arc-sine distributed (the stationary distribution of $\sin$). Get this wrong and the network emits pure noise — a pitfall I encountered during development and fixed only after carefully re-reading Appendix A of the original paper.

The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis

Frankle & Carlin's 2018 preprint (published at ICLR 2019, where it won a best paper award) articulated a deceptively simple observation: within any large randomly-initialized network, there exists a small subnetwork that, when reset to its original initialization values and trained in isolation, matches the performance of the full network. They called these subnetworks "winning tickets" because they had "won the initialization lottery" — their initial weights happened to occupy a geometry in parameter space that enables fast, effective learning.

The winning tickets we find have won the initialization lottery: their connections have initial weights that make training particularly effective.

— Frankle & Carlin, ICLR 2019

The mechanism for finding them — Iterative Magnitude Pruning (IMP) with weight rewinding — is elegantly procedural: train to convergence, remove lowest-magnitude weights, rewind surviving weights back to their initial values, repeat. The rewinding step is the crux. Without it, you're just compressing a trained network; with it, you're identifying which initial weight configurations are structurally privileged.

What makes this particularly interesting for INRs is the regime difference. LTH was established on classification tasks with discrete labels. INRs are pure regression problems — the "label" at each coordinate is the full-resolution pixel value. The network has no class boundaries to exploit, no discrete clusters to align with. It must encode a continuous function from scratch. Whether the lottery ticket phenomenon persists in this regime was, until this work, an open question.

Related Work

Frankle et al. (2020) later showed that IMP's requirement for exact initialization rewinding weakens in larger networks — "late rewinding" to an early training checkpoint often suffices. For small-scale INRs with few parameters, exact rewinding (to epoch 0) is necessary and sufficient. The cleaner the setup, the more the original hypothesis holds.

03 — Methodology

The Experimental Architecture

The SIREN Backbone

I used a 5-layer MLP with 256 hidden units at each layer, an input dimension of 2 (normalized pixel coordinates in $[-1, 1]^2$) and output dimension of 3 (RGB, in $[-1, 1]$). The first layer uses $\omega_0 = 30$ to set the base frequency; subsequent hidden layers inherit this factor. The output layer is a plain linear map — no sine, no clamp — allowing unconstrained gradient flow during training, with clamping applied only at reconstruction time.

Total parameters: 2×256+256 + 3×(256×256+256) + 256×3+3 = 198,915. Small enough to run entirely in VRAM, large enough that the LTH dynamics are genuinely interesting. The target: Leonardo da Vinci's Mona Lisa, resized to 256×256 and normalized. I chose this image deliberately — it has a rich mix of low-frequency gradients (the sky, skin) and high-frequency detail (the veil, background foliage), making the frequency-collapse mechanism maximally visible.

The Three Pruning Strategies

At each of 18 iterations, after 1,000 training epochs and Adam optimization at $\text{lr}=10^{-4}$ with cosine annealing, I applied one of three pruning functions to 20% of remaining active weights:

Strategy Rule Hypothesis
Winning Ticket Prune lowest $|w_i|$ Small weights are noise; large weights encode signal
Random Ticket Prune uniform random $20\%$ Control: does structure matter, or is any $N\%$ sufficient?
Losing Ticket Prune highest $|w_i|$ High-magnitude weights are load-bearing in SIREN

After each pruning step, surviving weights are rewound to their exact epoch-0 values — the true LTH protocol. This is implemented by maintaining a frozen copy of the initial state dict and overwriting module.weight_orig (PyTorch's internal pruning target) with the masked initial weights: $\theta_{\text{orig}} \leftarrow m \odot \theta_0$.

$$\text{Remaining}(k) = (1 - p)^k \qquad p = 0.20, \quad k \in \{1,\ldots,18\}$$
Total iterations: $k = \lceil \log(0.02) / \log(0.80) \rceil = 18$, reaching ${\approx}1.8\%$ remaining.

Critically: the "20% of remaining" formulation means pruning follows an exponential decay schedule, not a linear one. By iteration 18 we're at 1.8% of original weights — roughly 3,580 parameters carrying the entire Mona Lisa. The PSNR results at this extreme tell us what is structurally essential versus decorative in the network's parametrization.

Implementation Detail

The losing ticket required special handling. PyTorch's built-in pruning methods offer no "prune largest" variant. The custom implementation sorts all active weights globally on CPU (to avoid GPU kthvalue instability on degenerate distributions of near-zero values), determines the threshold at position $n_\text{keep}$ in the sorted array, and applies prune.custom_from_mask per-layer. Crucially, already-pruned zeros are excluded from the magnitude pool — otherwise they dominate and artificially inflate the threshold.

The Winner: Resilient to the End

The winning ticket starts at 47.3 dB — essentially perfect reconstruction. Its decay is remarkably slow: still above 40 dB at 32.8% remaining weights, above 35 dB at just 4.4%, and landing at 32.7 dB with only 2.3% of its original parameters. For context, 30 dB is the informal threshold for "perceptually acceptable" image quality. The winning ticket is still above this threshold at extreme sparsity.

What does this mean? That the Mona Lisa — all its sfumato gradients, its enigmatic expression, its Renaissance chiaroscuro — can be encoded in roughly 3,600 floating-point numbers and a connectivity pattern, down from 198,915. This is an approximately 55× compression of the network, with perceptually reasonable output quality. In sparse COO format (storing only non-zero weights with their indices), this translates to roughly 27 KB versus the original ~781 KB state dict.

The Random Ticket: Logarithmic Decay

The random ticket behaves exactly as you'd expect from information theory: smooth, monotonic decay. It stays surprisingly competitive at moderate sparsity (above 35 dB until around 10% remaining), then falls off rapidly below 5%. This is the baseline against which winning and losing tickets are compared — and it demonstrates that structure matters. The winning ticket consistently outperforms the random ticket at every sparsity level, confirming that the LTH is not a tautology (any sparse network is fine) but a genuine property of the initialization-magnitude alignment.

The Loser: Instantaneous Collapse

The losing ticket's curve is the most striking feature of this entire experiment. It begins at 47.6 dB — actually the highest starting point, noise variation aside. Then, at 80% remaining weights (just the first pruning), it drops to 29.3 dB. By 64% remaining, it's at 11.5 dB — the PSNR of a blank gray screen — where it flatlines for all subsequent iterations.

This is not gradual. It is a step function. The removal of 20% of the highest-magnitude weights causes the network to emit noise from which it never recovers across 18,000 additional training epochs. This is the frequency collapse: the entire signal-encoding capacity of the SIREN is destroyed in a single pruning round.

Key Finding

The winner-loser PSNR gap at 64% remaining is 34.5 dB. This is not a marginal experimental effect — it is the difference between a recognizable painting and white noise. No cherry-picking or hyperparameter tuning produces this; it is a structural consequence of how SIREN encodes information in its weight magnitudes.

05 — Discussion

Why the Loser Fails Catastrophically

The losing ticket's behavior requires explanation. In a standard ReLU network, pruning high-magnitude weights causes degradation — but gradual degradation, not instant collapse. Losing tickets in ReLU networks are bad; in SIRENs, they are dead.

SIREN Weights as Frequency Carriers

Consider what a SIREN weight matrix $\mathbf{W}_i$ actually does. The $j$-th output of layer $i$ computes $\sin(\omega_0 (\mathbf{W}_i \mathbf{x})_j + b_j)$. The magnitude of $(\mathbf{W}_i \mathbf{x})_j$ determines the amplitude of the oscillation at that node. Large weights → large pre-activation values → the sine oscillates across a wider range of its domain. Small weights → the sine barely moves from zero.

Through composition of $k$ such layers, the SIREN constructs a superposition of high-order trigonometric polynomials. The specific frequencies that can be represented are determined by which weight combinations produce constructive interference across layers. The high-magnitude weights are the primary oscillators — they set the dominant frequencies that give the reconstruction its edges and textures. The small-magnitude weights are the fine-tuning residuals.

When you prune the 20% largest weights globally, you don't just reduce the network's capacity. You remove the nodes that generate the highest-frequency components of the internal representation. What remains is a network of sub-harmonics — a SIREN that can only produce low-frequency hum. And crucially, no amount of retraining can recover those frequencies, because the rewound initial weights for those positions are also small (having survived as small weights from the original random initialization).

$$\Phi_\text{loser}(\mathbf{x}) \approx \sum_{k \in \mathcal{S}_\text{small}} c_k \sin(\omega_k \cdot \mathbf{x} + \psi_k)$$
After losing-ticket pruning, only low-amplitude basis functions remain. The effective bandwidth of the representation collapses to the lowest frequencies — insufficient to reconstruct any meaningful texture.

Why ReLU Networks Don't Suffer This

In a ReLU network, large weights don't have the same carrier role. A large weight on a ReLU node simply shifts the decision boundary. The signal is encoded in the pattern of active vs. inactive units, not in the magnitude of any individual weight. Removing the highest-magnitude weights reorganizes but doesn't annihilate the frequency content of the representation — ReLU networks have no frequency content to destroy in the first place; they encode signals through piecewise linear boundaries.

This is a deep architectural difference, not a hyperparameter sensitivity. The SIREN's power comes from its frequency-rich representation; its vulnerability to losing-ticket pruning is the flip side of that same coin.

Implications for NeRF and 3D Representation

The practical stakes here extend well beyond image compression. Neural Radiance Fields (NeRFs) use coordinate-MLPs structurally similar to SIRENs (often with positional encodings playing the role of SIREN's initialization). Recent work on NeRF compression — including KiloNeRF, Instant-NGP, and TensoRF — has focused on architecture redesign. The LTH perspective suggests a complementary approach: prune the NeRF itself, with rewinding. If a 1.8%-sparse SIREN can represent a 2D image with 32.7 dB PSNR, a sparse NeRF might represent 3D scenes with dramatically fewer parameters than current methods assume necessary.

The losing ticket experiment also has a practical negative implication: magnitude-based pruning on SIRENs must be done carefully}. If you prune high-magnitude weights — as some aggressive pruning schedules do when removing "outliers" — you risk frequency collapse rather than smooth compression. The safest pruning strategies for periodic-activation networks are IMP (lowest magnitude) or random pruning, never highest-magnitude removal.

Open Question

An intriguing follow-up: does the winning ticket found here transfer across images? Morcos et al. (2019) showed that lottery tickets found on CIFAR-10 sometimes transfer to CIFAR-100. For INRs, transferability would mean something even stranger — that the same sparse connectivity structure can be reused to represent entirely different natural signals. This seems improbable (each image has different frequency content), but the connectivity structure of the winning ticket might reveal universal properties of how SIRENs decompose 2D signals.

What the Winning Ticket Is Really Preserving

Looking at the winning ticket reconstructions in the lab above, a pattern emerges. At 5% remaining weights, the reconstruction loses fine detail — the intricate lace of the veil, the subtle texture of the background rocks — but preserves the macro-structure: the silhouette, the dominant color gradients, the facial geometry. This is not accidental.

The small-magnitude weights that the winning ticket preserves are precisely those responsible for the low-frequency backbone of the signal. They're small in magnitude because the SIREN's initialization scheme deliberately scales hidden-layer weights by $1/\omega_0$ — small at initialization, they correspond to low-frequency components that are easy to learn and cheap to represent. The winning ticket is, in essence, the Fourier low-pass filter of the full network, and IMP happens to be the procedure that isolates it.

06 — Compression Analysis

The Space-Time Tradeoff

One of the original motivations for this experiment was the question of whether INRs can be practically compressed. The answer is emphatically yes, with a caveat: the compression is theoretical rather than practical under standard dense computation.

Important Clarification

As shown in the benchmark results, PyTorch's unstructured pruning does NOT automatically produce inference speedups. The pruning mask is applied as $\mathbf{W}_\text{eff} = \mathbf{W}_\text{orig} \odot M$, which remains a dense operation. Real latency gains require hardware-aware sparse kernels (e.g., CUDA cuSPARSE, or custom CUTLASS kernels) or structural pruning (removing entire rows/columns). The compression numbers below refer to storage, not compute.

In COO (Coordinate) sparse format, each non-zero weight requires storing its value (float32) and its index (int32) — 8 bytes versus 4 bytes for a dense weight. The break-even point is 50% density; below that, sparse is smaller. At 2.3% density, we store roughly 4,575 parameters as (index, value) pairs — total storage ≈ 36.6 KB, compared to 781 KB dense. A practical 21× reduction.

07 — Reflections

What This Tells Us About Neural Computation

The most intellectually interesting outcome of this experiment is not the compression ratio — it's what the three ticket types reveal about how SIRENs are internally organized.

The existence of a winning ticket at 2.3% density implies that 97.7% of this network's parameters are, in some sense, redundant given the right initialization. But this redundancy is not waste — it's the price of the optimization landscape. The dense network's overparameterization creates a smooth loss surface that gradient descent can traverse; the sparse winning ticket, found only after training the dense network, cannot be discovered by directly training the sparse architecture from scratch. The dense network is a scaffolding for identifying the sparse solution.

This is a profound statement about what learning is doing at a fine-grained level. It's not just fitting parameters to data — it's identifying, from a combinatorially large space of possible sparse structures, the specific connectivity that allows learning to work. The Mona Lisa is not in any 2.3% of this network. It's in a very specific 2.3% — the one that, when rewound to initialization, happens to sit in a favorable region of optimization geometry.

Dense networks are the lottery in which winning tickets are sold. You cannot buy only the winning ticket; you must buy all of them and then discover which one won.

— paraphrasing Frankle's own reflections on the hypothesis

The losing ticket tells the complementary story. High-magnitude weights are not "more important" in a general sense — they're specifically important to SIREN's frequency decomposition. This is architecture-specific. In a transformer, high-magnitude weights in attention heads might encode something entirely different. The "carrier wave" interpretation of SIREN's large weights is a hypothesis worth testing more rigorously, perhaps with direct Fourier analysis of the activation statistics at each layer.

Finally, the random ticket's logarithmic decay suggests that SIRENs have a degree of graceful degradation — you can remove up to 80-90% of weights randomly and still maintain reasonable quality. This is reassuring for robustness: SIREN representations are not brittle by default. They become brittle only when you specifically target their frequency-carrying architecture.
